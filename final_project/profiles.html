My First Knife

My first knife was given to me as a gift on or shortly after my 8th birthday. I was excited to use it in Cub Scouts. I am sure I was required to pass some sort of safety knowledge test to be permitted to carry and use a knife. The Diamond Edge brand This pocket knife originally came with side scales covered in a black celluloid-like plastic material. This material soon cracked, so I tore it all off. One of my first memories using this knife was cutting string from bales of hay on my grandfather’s farm. I also have distinct memories of using this knife contrary to my early instruction in knife safety. I had a leather belt and needed to make an extra hole. I placed the belt on my desk, in my room and began to use the main blade to bore a hole. The blade closed on the end of my left thumb, parallel to the bone. I still carry the scar of that injury. The experience has changed how I hold and use knives (and care for wounds) to this day.

  • Blade lock mechanism: none
  • Manufacturer/Model: Diamond Edge
  • Blades: one traditional spear type blade, awl, can opener, screwdriver/bottle opener combination
  • Scales/handle: black textured celluloid plastic (original)
  • Sheath: n/a
  • Blade length: 2 5/8”
  • Overall length: 6 1/4”

Brazilian Souvenir

I purchased this knife as a souvenir before returning home in 1992 from a two-year service experience in Brazil. I got it from a typical souvenir shop. Knives like this were commonly made by street artisans in northern Brazil for tourists. It is a cheap kitchen knife covered in epoxy putty, colored with local/natural pigments and decorated with local natural materials. Seeds (red), bones, fish teeth, and a snail shell adorn the sculpted epoxy. The sheath and handle are covered with snake skin. Happily, the US Customs service had nothing to say about it when I reentered the US in Florida. Simpler times. Unfortunately, the same was not the case for a co-traveler who decided to try and bring a small, beautiful mauve colored boa constrictor to the US, tied in a sock.

  • Blade lock mechanism: n/a
  • Manufacturer/Model: Mundial
  • Blades: single, fixed
  • Scales/handle: sculpted epoxy and snakeskin
  • Sheath: sculpted epoxy and snakeskin
  • Blade length: 5 1/2”
  • Overall length: 11”

The First Knife I Bought for Myself

I bought this knife when I was fourteen and in the Varsity Scout program. I felt that I needed a cooler knife for camping and something light for hiking. Similar styles with brass and wood scales were very popular among my peers, larger being more desirable. I bought it at the local Army/Navy surplus store. It came in a forest green and black sheath (not pictured) with a Velcro closure flap. I was so excited to have my new knife. I took it home, sharpened it to a fine edge. It was sharp enough to shave hair from my forearm. I went outside to test it. Almost immediately I decided to throw the knife and stick the blade into our wood playhouse. Throw the knife? What was I thinking? It was NOT a good idea. An eighth inch length at the tip of the blade bent 90° as the metal entered the wood paneling and the momentum of the throw forced the rest of the knife to one side. My heart skipped a beat. I berated myself for being so stupid with my brand-new knife. I gently hammered the tip back into place and never did that again. I used this knife for years as a teen and young adult, especially for camping.

  • Body style: folder
  • Blade lock mechanism: lockback
  • Manufacturer/Model: EdgeLite / Explorer
  • Blades: single, clip point
  • Scales/handle: olive green textured nylon
  • Sheath: nylon, forest green and black
  • Blade length: 3”
  • Overall length: 7”

The Hunting Knife I Forged

I forged this knife in 2021. The occasion was my 50th birthday. I traveled to be near my brothers and new son-in-law. I arranged to make hunting knives with them at the Stone Fox Forge in Orem, Utah. The blacksmith gave each of us a steel blank band. We heated the metal in small propane forges, until they glowed yellow. He taught us how to hammer out the shape of the blade and tang in a stepwise manner. After we were satisfied, the blacksmith helped us grind and sharpen the blades. The handles are wrapped in paracord. To finish off the evening, we took turns filming ourselves slice through water bottles with the newly formed blades. We continued that activity at home for days. It was a ton of fun. The experience was unforgettable.

  • Body style: fixed blade hunting knife
  • Blade lock mechanism: n/a
  • Manufacturer/Model: me
  • Blades: single, trailing point
  • Scales/handle: wrapped red paracord
  • Sheath: n/a
  • Blade length: 4 1/2”
  • Overall length: 9 1/2”

A Hunting Knife from a Kit

I assembled and finished this knife during a summer wood shop program when I was fifteen. The program gave participants an opportunity to do two projects. I had taken wood shop as an elective class during the school year. I was very excited to learn one available project was to ‘make’ a hunting knife from a list of available kits. I chose the larges available and paid $30 or so to buy the kit. The woodworking aspect of the project was simply to shape and finish the handle. The handle wood came with the kit, in a left and right half. I cut the handle to my desired shape with a band saw. At the time, deeply scalloped handle grips were popular. I was concerned that I wouldn’t get the contours just right for my hand, so I kept it simple. After the handle was shaped, I glued the halves together, sanded, and stained it. Assembly was very simple. The tang of the blade inserts through the brass bolster, into a channel in the handle and onto the bolster. A round brass nut secures the whole assembly. The sheath shown in the picture was made at the same time, and came as part of the kit. My kind but stern shop teacher, Mr. Ohlwheiler had some brass brads in the shop. He showed me how to use them to strengthen the corners of the sheath where the stitching ended. While I chose this knife for its size, this same characteristic has kept me from using the knife much. As an adult I decided to give this blade a good sharpening. I sharpened both the bottom edge and top swage edge. Being unaccustomed to a sharp upper edge, I soon cut myself. I decided to dull the top edge and prevent further incidents.

  • Sheath: handmade, tooled leather
  • Blade length: 6 1/4”
  • Overall length: 10 3/4”

Refurbished Hunting Knife

I found this knife while deer hunting on my grandfather’s mountain property, “Gooseberry”. Did we ‘get our deer’? No. The blade was sitting in the dirt along the top of a ridge bordering the eastern property line. We often found fragments of Native American pottery along the same ridge. When I found it, the knife was severely corroded. Its tang had been covered with rounded layers of leather and some other material to form a handle. This had all but completely rotted away. There was an iron bolster at the end of the handle. I took the knife to the same summer woodworking program I made the kit hunting knife in. There I refurbished the knife. I chose Zebra wood to make the handle. Brass brads to secure the handle were again furnished by my shop teacher. I tried to make a slightly more sculpted handle on this knife. I remember taking time to determine where my fingers would line up along its length.

  • Blades: drop point
  • Scales/handle: Zebra wood
  • Sheath: n/a
  • Blade length: 3 5/8”
  • Overall length: 7 1/2”

My Latest Purchase

I bought this knife in 2021, as a birthday present for myself. I noticed the knife while browsing casually online, and fell in love with its shape and construction. The blade has a 1/4 inch flare at the base of the blade that acts as a small finger guard when open. When closed, this same construct protrudes from the back of the handle, providing a lever to assist opening the blade. The manufacturer offers this knife with several options for scale color and blade finish. This type of construction is sometimes referred to as a ‘flipper’, because it has a design feature which helps the user open the blade easily. This knife inspired me to shape my forged hunting knife with a similar trailing point blade.

  • Blades: trailing point
  • Scales/handle: G10 epoxy fiberglass
  • Sheath: n/a
  • Blade length: 3 1/2”
  • Overall length: 8 1/4”

Watermelon Slicer

I bought this machete as a present for my Dad when I was about 16. It came from the same Army/Navy surplus store where I bought my first knife. My teenage mind thought it would be extra cool to give him a large knife to cut watermelons with. It was summer. We ate watermelons aplenty. Why not? I think he tried it once, but let’s face it: the blade is nowhere as convenient as a kitchen knife when you are preparing food. Who wants to go out to the shed or garage to retrieve the machete just to slice a melon. Beside that, the blade isn’t ground in the same way a slicing knife is ground. It really isn’t very wieldy and tends to slice a curved path through melons. Yes, I tried. Oh well. In later years my Dad gave it back to me. You may guess from the picture that I no longer slice food with it. I use it every chance I get to prune bushes and whack weeds. Incidentally, in Brazil I occasionally observed people using machetes to ‘mow’ sections of lawn. The common working model typically was around 20-24” in length. I purchased one made by Tramontina for myself to bring back from Brazil. Alas, it was too big for my suitcase. I gave it away. Do I want another? Yes, yes I do.

  • Manufacturer/Model: unknown
  • Blades: spear point
  • Scales/handle: plastic
  • Sheath: canvas (not shown)
  • Blade length: 17”
  • Overall length: 22”

Swiss Army Pioneer

This knife is my current favorite. I suppose you could say I purchased this knife. I came across it for the first time in the basement of the first house I bought. I was in the process of finishing the basement. The knife had been hidden on top of heating ducts in the basement ceiling. I just happened to reach up and feel it there one day. Who knows how long it had been there or under what circumstances it came to rest? I’m glad I found it. I carry it every day. The quality of construction is just as one would expect from Victorinox. The can opener and awl have sharpened edges. The blade is thick along the spine and tapered toward the tip. The handle has a small steel ring to accommodate a lanyard. As seen in the picture, I use a piece of paracord tied with a diamond knot as my lanyard. Similar models are available currently in Victorinox’s Pioneer line.

  • Body style: folding canoe pocket knife
  • Blade lock mechanism: none
  • Manufacturer/Model: Victorinox / Pioneer or Soldier
  • Blades: spear point, awl, combination can opener / small screwdriver, combination large screwdriver and bottle opener
  • Scales/handle: textured Alox (aluminum), red finish
  • Sheath: n/a
  • Blade length: 3 3/4”
  • Overall length: 8 3/8”
